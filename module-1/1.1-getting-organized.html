The line you drew is called the entity boundary. It decides what goes into the accounting records and what stays out. Every time an accountant records something, they have already made a judgment: does this belong to the entity (the shop) or not?

The boundary isn’t always obvious. If you use your personal car to pick up parts for the shop, did the car cross the boundary for that trip? Accounting systems handle this with rules — but those rules are built on top of the same idea you just discovered by sorting ten things into two bins.

Notice also what the entity boundary does not capture: you. Your knowledge of how to true a wheel, when a frame is worth fixing, which wrench to grab — that’s arguably the most important resource the shop has. But it isn’t a thing the boundary can hold. We’ll return to this puzzle later.

Step 2

Things aren’t enough — you also need promises

You’ve listed what you have. But there’s something the list of things doesn’t capture. Last month, a customer paid a deposit for a custom build that isn’t finished yet. Your parts supplier sent a bill you haven’t paid. The landlord expects rent at the end of the week. These aren’t things — they’re promises. You owe something.

A complete picture of your shop has two sides: what you have, and what you owe. A shop that has $5,000 worth of equipment but owes $8,000 to suppliers is in a very different position than one that holds the same equipment free and clear. If you only track things, you are missing half the picture.

Something you have agreed to do or pay, where the doing or paying comes later. When your supplier ships parts before you pay, you have made a promise. When a customer pays a deposit before you finish the work, you have made a promise to them.

A statement from a supplier or service provider telling you how much you owe and when to pay it. Receiving a bill doesn’t mean you’ve paid yet — it means you now have an obligation recorded.

Money paid in advance for something to be received or done later. When a customer gives you a deposit, you have cash — but you also have an obligation. You owe the customer the completed work. Until the job is done, the deposit represents a promise you haven’t fulfilled yet.

Someone who pays you for goods or services. When a customer pays a deposit, the shop receives cash (a thing you have) but also takes on an obligation to complete the work (a promise you owe).

A business you buy from. When a supplier delivers parts before you pay, the shop has new inventory (a thing you have) and also a new obligation to pay the supplier (a promise you owe).

Two lists. One complete picture. Every accounting system — from a small repair shop to a global corporation — is built on these two lists: what the entity has, and what it owes. Accountants call them assets and liabilities. You just built the foundation of a balance sheet.

When an entity owes more than it has, the difference is called negative equity — or, informally, being “underwater.” This doesn’t necessarily mean the business will fail immediately, but it is a warning sign that the entity’s resources are not sufficient to cover its obligations.

Understanding this gap is one of the core reasons financial statements exist. A lender, a partner, or a potential buyer all want to know the same thing: does this entity have more than it owes — and by how much? The two lists you just made are the beginning of that answer.
