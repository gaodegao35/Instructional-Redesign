Three Nonprofits, One Donation

You still have $25K. This time, you are considering a donation rather than an investment. You want to support a nonprofit that serves youth, and you have identified three candidates: Harbor Youth and Family Support, Bridgeway Counseling Center, and Northline Youth Services.

You will review their records in stages, just as you did with the three businesses in Part 1. At each step, you will consider what the information helps you understand and what you would still want to know before making a donation decision.

The same accounting identity still organizes the records. But because nonprofits do not have owners, the residual is called Net Assets rather than Equity. As the records unfold, you will see how nonprofit statements organize information differently from for-profit statements.

What you'll discover
  • • That the nonprofit balance sheet is built around the same identity from Part 1 — the residual just carries a different name.
  • • That the residual is shown in two parts tracking whether donor-imposed restrictions still apply — a split the for-profit side has no analog for.
  • • That the same evidence test from Part 1 governs what enters the records — and real resources can end up on the outside of that line.
A note on simplifications

Real financial statements include more detail than shown here, and the rules behind them are more complex.

This exercise keeps the statements simple so you can focus on the basic structure: the accounting identity, the evidence needed to include items in the records, and the role of statements that explain changes over time.

The goal is to carry the logic from Part 1 into a nonprofit setting and see how nonprofit statements organize information differently.

Step 3: Net Assets Breakdown
Your previous choice

The balance sheets now show how each nonprofit's Total Net Assets are classified. In this example, Total Net Assets are divided into two categories: Without Donor Restrictions and With Donor Restrictions.

  • • Without Donor Restrictions means the nonprofit can use those funds for its general mission and operations. These amounts may come from donations with no donor limits, or from restricted funds after the donor's requirement has been met.
  • • With Donor Restrictions means the donor has limited how or when the funds or resources can be used. The nonprofit has received them, but it must follow the donor's restriction until that restriction is satisfied.
  • • When a restriction is satisfied, the amount is released from restriction. In the records, it moves out of With Donor Restrictions and into Without Donor Restrictions.

For Bridgeway, the $400 listed With Donor Restrictions is tied to a building donated for adolescent counseling services. Because that donor restriction has not yet been satisfied, the $400 remains in the restricted category.

What Changed in Net Assets?
Your previous choice

The balance sheet is a snapshot of the accounting identity at one point in time. To understand how Net Assets changed during a period, nonprofits use a statement called the Statement of Activities.

In Part 1, the income statement helped explain the change in Retained Earnings. The Statement of Activities plays a similar role for nonprofits. It explains changes in Net Assets, including changes in Without Donor Restrictions and With Donor Restrictions.

Two kinds of changes can appear:

  1. 1. Activities that change Total Net Assets — These include revenues, expenses, gains, and losses. They change the total amount of Net Assets.
  2. 2. Releases from donor restrictions — When a donor restriction is satisfied, the amount is released from restriction. In the records, it moves from With Donor Restrictions to Without Donor Restrictions. This changes how Net Assets are classified, but it does not change Total Net Assets.

The Statement of Activities shows both kinds of changes in one place.

Do Volunteer Hours Enter the Records?

The same logic from Part 1 still applies: not everything valuable to an organization automatically appears in the financial records.

For nonprofits, this question often comes up with donated labor, also called contributed services. Volunteers may provide work that is essential to the mission, but many volunteer hours are not recorded as contribution revenue or expense.

In general, donated services enter the records only when they meet specific accounting conditions. They may be recorded when:

  1. 1. They create or improve a nonfinancial asset — for example, volunteers help build or renovate a shelter.
  2. 2. They require specialized professional skills that the nonprofit would otherwise need to purchase — for example, a lawyer provides pro bono legal services, or an accountant donates audit services.

The Evidence Test: Collection Capitalization

One more place the convention asks a recognition question. Northline holds a community-history archive — oral histories, photographs, and memorabilia from the neighborhoods it serves. The archive is used in Northline's heritage-education program with teens.

Items like these can qualify as a collection — items held for exhibition, education, or research; preserved; and governed by a policy that any sale proceeds must be reinvested in the collection. Northline's archive qualifies.

Capitalize means record as an asset on the balance sheet. When an organization does not capitalize an item, the item does not appear in the accounting records — even though the organization still holds it.

The convention allows an organization holding a collection to choose whether to capitalize it. Northline has chosen not to capitalize its archive.

Records show less than the entity has

Northline's balance sheet shows exactly what Harbor's shows — $500 in cash, no liabilities, $500 in Net Assets Without Donor Restrictions. But Northline also holds a community archive that Harbor does not. The records are silent about it. This is the convention giving an organization permission to leave a real asset — even a major one — entirely outside the books.

What the Structure Shows

Four things this exercise shows about the structure of nonprofit financial statements — and how each one relates back to Part 1.

  1. 1. Same identity, different name for the residual. Assets − Liabilities = Net Assets for nonprofits, the same way Assets − Liabilities = Equity for for-profits. The identity holds at every point in time, by construction.
  2. 2. The residual is shown in subparts — but the question the subparts answer is different. Part 1's Equity split into Contributed Capital and Retained Earnings — a "where did it come from" question. Part 2's Net Assets splits into Without Donor Restrictions and With Donor Restrictions — tracking whether donor-imposed restrictions on the use of resources still apply.
  3. 3. The explanatory statement does one extra job. Part 1's income statement decomposed value changes to Retained Earnings. The Statement of Activities decomposes value changes to Net Assets and shows reclassifications between the two restriction categories. The release row zeros out in the Total column — it moves dollars sideways, not up or down.
  4. 4. The evidence test still governs — and the nonprofit setting puts it on display. Most contributed services stay outside the records. A collection an organization chooses not to capitalize never enters the records at all. In each case a real economic event exists; the convention draws its evidence line somewhere that leaves it out.

The deeper point

For-profit and nonprofit financial statements share a common skeleton: an identity at a point in time, a residual shown in subparts that carry specific information, and an explanatory statement that decomposes how the residual changed during the period. But that skeleton is tailored to the situation — to the kind of organization being reported on, and to the kind of information useful for that kind of organization. The two tutorials share a structure and diverge in the details that matter.
