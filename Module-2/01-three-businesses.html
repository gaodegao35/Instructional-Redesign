Three Businesses, One Decision

In Module 1 you built the recordkeeping system from the inside out — identify the entity, list its Assets and Liabilities, and the residual falls out as Equity (or Net Assets, for an organization no one owns). A − L = E by construction. Time to apply it.

You have $25k. You can invest it in one of three for-profit businesses, or donate it to a nonprofit. This first part of the exercise looks at the three for-profits. The donation option comes up in the next part.

Each business started from nothing exactly one year ago. You will see their records, step by step, and work out how the pieces fit together.

What you'll discover
  • • That the balance sheet is constructed around a single identity.
  • • What different information the two sides of a balance sheet carry.
  • • How a second statement explains the change in one part of the right side.
A note on simplifications

Real financial statements have many more lines than what appears in this exercise, and the rules that govern what can go on them are more detailed than what is covered here.

How Did It Get on the Records?

Business C's records include two connected entries: a $100 asset labeled Great Idea on the balance sheet, and a $100 line on the income statement labeled Owner's Estimated Gain from Great Idea. The income statement line reports $100 of Net Income, which explains the $100 shown in Retained Earnings.

Here is the backstory. The owner believes the idea could help the business create value in the future and assigns it a value of $100. But no outside party has paid for the idea, bought it, or licensed it. No money changed hands, and no exchange took place. The owner simply declared a value and entered the numbers into the records.

Do you think Business C should be allowed to record the Great Idea as a $100 asset and report a related gain as income?
Briefly explain why.

Correct.

Business C should not record the Great Idea as a $100 asset or report a related gain as income based only on the owner's estimate.

For an item to enter the records, there needs to be enough evidence that it exists as an accounting item and that its financial effect can be measured. Here, no outside party bought, licensed, or paid for the idea, and no exchange took place.

The idea may still be valuable, but the records do not provide enough evidence to include it in the financial statements. This rule keeps self-declared values off the records, but it can also leave out genuinely valuable things, such as in-house knowledge, customer relationships, or a brand built inside the business.

What the Structure Shows

Three things this exercise shows about the structure of for-profit financial statements.

  1. 1. An identity holds the balance sheet together. Assets − Liabilities = Equity, at every point in time, by construction.
  2. 2. The residual gets broken into subparts. Contributed Capital and Retained Earnings answer different questions about where the Equity balance came from. Without the breakdown, the balance sheet can't carry those distinctions.
  3. 3. A second statement explains how the residual changed. The income statement tells the story behind Retained Earnings. Two businesses with the same Retained Earnings balance can have very different income statements behind them — and the structure makes those differences visible.

The deeper point

Financial statements are not a single report. They are a connected set. The balance sheet presents an identity at a point in time. The income statement explains how a part of that identity — Retained Earnings — changed during the period. The structure is built to carry information that no single statement could carry alone.
