Every time something happens that might affect the balance sheet, the system requires three decisions:

Recognition, Classification, and Quantification

The three decisions behind every recorded transaction.

Recognition — does this event enter the system?
+

Does this event get recorded at all? Not everything does. A company signs a promising contract, but no goods have been delivered and no cash has changed hands. The system may not recognize it yet. Recognition is the gatekeeper — it determines what enters the system and what stays outside the boundary. As we saw in the conventions discussion, the recognition rules are restrictive: they require evidence, usually in the form of a transaction, before something enters the books. This is why some of an entity's most important economic resources — its workforce, its brand, its strategic position — may never appear on a balance sheet.

Classification — where does it land in the identity?
+

If the event is recognized, which elements of the identity does it affect? Does it change assets? Liabilities? Equities? More than one? Classification is where you decide where the effects land. A bank loan increases both cash (an asset) and the loan payable (a liability). A sale might increase a receivable (asset) and increase equities through revenue. Getting the classification right is essential — it determines what the balance sheet shows and where.

Quantification — what number goes in?
+

What numbers go in? This is often the hardest step, and it is where conventions do the most work. If the entity bought equipment for cash, the amount is straightforward — the transaction price anchors it. But if the entity must estimate the worth of a warranty obligation, or allocate the cost of a building over its useful life, judgment enters the picture. Different conventions produce different numbers, and the numbers shape the financial story the system tells.

These three steps — recognize, classify, quantify — are what it means to "work the identity." Every transaction, every adjustment, every recording goes through this sequence, and every one must leave the identity in balance.
